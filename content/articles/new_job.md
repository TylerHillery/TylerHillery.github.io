#### **Career Background**  
My career aspirations have changed a lot over the course of college and early on into my career. My original plan was to become a civil engineer until I took a civil engineering course my senior year in high school & knew it wasn't for me. (Course was only offered to seniors who completed several engineering pre reqs).

After that, I decided to attend a 2 year school to obtain my associates degree to give me some time to figure out what I wanted to do in my future before transferring to a 4 year school. My original plan was to become a math major with an actuarial emphasis. After taking calculus 2 I decided that was enough math for me...

I then defaulted on a degree in Finance for a couple of reasons:

1. Learning about finance is valuable knowledge to have no matter what career I end up choosing as it applies to everyday life. 


2. I like how a degree in finance doesn't limit me in any particularly industry as every business has a finance department.

3. Ever since I was young I was always interested in personal finance & investments. I remember a time at a baseball game my parents gave me money to buy candy for myself at the concession stand & I ended up pocketing the money. I was obsessed with saving & being able to deposit money at our local bank to earn "moola" bucks.  

After setting on a degree in finance I had to narrow down what it was I wanted to do. The two main career paths I was considering was either investment management or financial planning. Which led me to do an internship with Northwestern Mutual as a financial rep (lesson learn.)
___

<div id="financialrep"></div>

#### **Financial Rep**
The summer after my sophomore year I decided to take an internship at Northwestern Mutual as a financial representative. I ended up quitting 3 months into the internship because I knew this *salesy* type of role wasn't for me. I also wasn't a fan of a commission based role especially for financial planning as I feel it creates a conflict of interest that makes it hard for the *advisor* to give unbiased advice.

After that experience I was turned off by the idea of becoming a financial advisor even though I respect a lot of the people in the profession. I feel financial literacy is a huge problem that needs to be solved and the financial advisory business tends to get a bad rap because of its history. I feel the industry as a whole is heading in the right direction with the push to fee-only financial advising. It's an industry that I still want to be apart but I need find a role that fits my skill set (more on that later).
___

#### **Analyst Intern**
I was set on working in investment management after my experience at NM. I started to look into studying for the CFA level 1 exam my last year in College. I needed to find a job though for my junior year to help pay for daily expenses & the retail industry wasn't cutting it for me. Luckily I found a posting for an *Analyst Intern* at Wausau Homes.

The position was a little vague on what I will be doing but little did I know this internship would forever change what I wanted to do. I worked in the marketing department primarily focusing on ways we could leverage data & technology to improve process efficiency. 

This involved a lot of work on improving our CRM system (sugar crm). I automated ways to track builder activity and created new dashboards for our builders to give them better ideas of their activity levels. This was the role where I learned a lot of excel skills. 

I created a spreadsheet where our builders would enter in how many houses they wanted to build each month and then it calculated how many customers they would need at each stage of the business process to meet these productivity levels. I was able to present this to upper management so they can look at the current activity of all of our builders to help forecast what kind of inventory they would need for upcoming months. 

This role felt like a natural fit for my personality. Where I could take my finance/business background combined with these technical skills to help drive decision making. I ended up doing a lot of research on what types of roles did this type of work & that is when I discovered the world of **Data Science & Analytics**. After that I was determined to get another internship where they had a more established analytics department to get some more mentorship on developing my technical skills.

<div id="investassoc"></div>

#### **Investments Associate**
My role of the investments associate was to help with [Back Office](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/backoffice.asp) operations. Things like trade settlement, asset reconciliation, mail, entering in transactions. It was not the most glamorous role but it afforded me the opportunity to work with other Portfolio Managers & CFA charter holders where I could ask questions and learn more about the Investment Management industry. I got exposure to valuable tools like the Bloomberg Terminal, PAM (investment accounting software).

 What I liked about the back office team was that we were responsible for the handling a lot of the data integration, management and reporting for the department. We were primarily a "Microsoft Shop" using Office 365 tools like Excel, & Access. We pushed these tools to their limits utilizing VBA, Power Query and pivot tables for a lot of reports and processes. We had several access databases that were more like GUI applications used for ETL & entering in data. The tooling may have been a little old school but the logic and methodologies we were implementing are better than most places I have ever been at.
___

<div id="investwrksys"></div>

#### **Investments Workflow & Systems Analyst**
Luckily after my temp position was ending a position opened up on the team as an Investments Workflow & Systems analyst. This role was primary responsible for finding ways we could leverage data & technology to improve process efficiency & accessability of information. I was able to work on several projects during my time there that greatly impacted out team

1. Built an Access Application called the "MessageBot" which would allow users to input data and export messages that would automatically get uploaded to our investment accounting software. e.g. Upload bond ratings that would come from Bloomberg Excel API and determine if any changes have been made. Create messages for changes and update the ratings in PAM. 

2. Developed a more robust report using Access, Excel (PowerQuery, PivotTables) that would reconcile our holdings between our accounting software, Bloomberg Terminal & our custody bank.

These are just a couple examples of the projects I was able to work on. I was really set on working a career in investment operations as I felt it combined two main interests of mine investments & technology. I even pursued the CFA charter, took & passed the level 1 exam. After the level 1 exam I had a realization. I was more & more interested in working with the technology and data & I felt at the time those were the skills I really needed to develope. 

I got my first exposure to programming with VBA when developing some of the Access applications. It was these projects that opened my eyes to what was possible with programming & I really wanted to learn more. I felt that in my current role I was being limited with the tooling & technology available. There was a total of about 15 people in the investments department and the only other person that knew these technical skills was my boss who I owe a great deal to for helping me get to where I am today. I thought it was time to look for other opportunities. I ended up getting an offer from Voya Finanical as a Sr. Investment Systems Analyst, that's when Sentry decided to create an Investments Data Analyst position to support my growth & interests.
___

<div id="investdata"></div>

#### **Investments Data Analyst**
Becoming a Data Analyst was surreal moment for me. I always wanted to work in the analytics department but felt that I didn't belong because of my finance background & lack of technical skills. What got me here was my determination to never stop learning. I have this innate curiosity in me that always wants to understand how things work. I am consistently asking **Why?** and **How?** My technical skills did not get me to this position it was my ability to learn, ask questions and understand the business. In my new role I was responsible with refactor the investments department to new tooling (SAS & Tableau). I was always tasked with new reporting need, for the investments group  that focuses on ESG (environment, social, governance) impact of our investments. 

In the beginning I did a lot of requirements gathering and documentation to get a better understanding of the access databases. During my time on the Investment Ops team I didn't do too much digging into how a lot of these views & tables were getting created in access, if it's not broke why fix it? After diving into some of these databases I felt I bit off more than I can chew. I was also in a unique position where I was now on the analytics team but I didn't really feel included because I was the only one supporting the investments department. No one else really knew about the investment operations.

 The main reason I decided to stay at Sentry was because I was looking to learn from my peers who are more experienced but instead I felt more isolated than ever before. I didn't feel fully accepted on the analytics team and now I was no longer part of investments. I'm sure it was hard for Sentry to quickly create this role for me but this was a tough point in my life. I felt really alone and had no where to go. I also feel like I had to keep up this perception that I knew what I was doing but that was far from the truth. Normally I don't have an issue admitting when I don't understand something but this was different.

I began to self reflect about what it was I wanted to do in my future. I knew it had something to do with data and programming but there are several different roles that you can do. I started to feel that I would enjoy it more as a data engineer focusing on the data pipelines, infrastructure & platform. To me that is where I see the most impact & value out of these data roles (analytics engineering is a close 2nd). I also find it to be more technically demanding which is something that I like. I knew that my skill level wasn't there yet to apply for data engineer roles. I have plans to start a masters program in analytics which I know will help with those skills but I wasn't sure I wanted to wait that long to move towards that career. So I decided to look at other roles that I can use as a stepping stone to become a data engineer. After I did some research it seemed that BI developer, Data Developer, SQL Developer, Associate or Jr Data Engineer were all roles that more aligned with the type of work I was interested. 

One thing that was important to me too was working in the investment management, wealth planning & financial services industry. I still find that space fascinating and I can leverage my background the best. I also considered working in tech focusing on tools that support the data ecosystem. 

After several job applications I ended up taking a Business Intelligence Developer position at Mutual of Omaha supporting their Income & Wealth Planning Department.
___

<div id="bidevelop"></div>

#### **Business Intelligence Developer**

There were several reasons that attracted me to this role

1. The type of work was more aligned with Data Engineering responsibilities such as developing ETL pipelines. It is a more gradual introduction because I will be using less code intensive tools such as SSIS and I am still developing reports & dashboarding but it is still a step in the right direction.

2. Mutual of Omaha offers tuition reimbursement which will help me pay for my masters degree. 

3. Mutual of Omaha has a big initiative right now in technology modernization and moving towards the cloud (AWS Glue, Lake Formation, Athena, Snowflake). To me I see this as opportunity to get involved with these projects. While our team IWP DMR (income & wealth planning data management reporting) is not technically directly involved with implementing these tools, that falls more on our information services team that has the data engineers. We still have been working with them on how we can move our processes to the cloud.

4. Being in the income & wealth planning is a good fit for me as this has been an industry I have been interested in awhile. I truly want to be one of the leading tech & data experts in this space. While Mutual of Omaha is more insurance focused I think it's a great start.  

I already have learned a lot my first couple weeks at Mutual Of Omaha & it has me excited for the future. There is a lot of work to be done with refactoring a lot of old tools they use such as Access, SAP Business Objects, SSIS. My career has been reinvigorated. I am great spot to grow as an aspiring data engineer. My application to GaTech's Online Master of Analytics has been submitted so fingers cross I will be accepted. My goal is to get promoted to data engineer in 2 years and graduate in 3 years.

<div id="conclusion"></div>

## **Conlusion** 
I am still early on into my career but I've learned a lot that helped me get to where I am today. I wouldn't have done it any differently. Could I have graduated with a CS major and went more the data engineering route right away? Sure I could have but then I wouldn't have all the knowledge of finance and business. 

Like I said before my technical skills are not what got me to where I am today. It's my willingness to learn what tool that is required for the task at hand. It's my non stem background that gives me perspective into how other non tech professionals work. It my curiosity and courage I have to ask questions to truly understand. It's my positive attitude and personality that I bring to meetings and 1:1 ones. It's my consistency and discipline I bring day in and day out.
